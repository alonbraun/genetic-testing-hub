A new sequencing methodology combining the breadth of whole-genome sequencing with the targeted depth of exome sequencing is drawing attention across the clinical genomics community. Published findings highlighted by Inside Precision Medicine this week describe how blended genome–exome sequencing can significantly reduce per-sample costs without introducing meaningful losses in data quality — a development that could reshape how laboratories approach routine and rare-disease sequencing workflows.

## The Technology

Blended genome–exome sequencing works by strategically merging the complementary strengths of two well-established approaches. Whole-genome sequencing provides comprehensive variant detection across coding and non-coding regions but carries a higher cost burden. Exome sequencing concentrates sequencing depth on protein-coding regions, where the majority of clinically actionable variants reside, at a lower price point. The blended methodology leverages enrichment strategies and computational frameworks to allocate sequencing resources more efficiently, achieving sufficient depth across clinically relevant regions while capturing broader genomic context elsewhere. The result is a workflow that avoids the all-or-nothing tradeoff laboratories have historically been forced to accept when selecting a sequencing strategy for a given patient population or research cohort.

## Clinical Context

The implications for clinical laboratories are considerable. Cost has remained one of the most persistent barriers to the widespread adoption of comprehensive genomic profiling, particularly in rare disease diagnostics, pediatric medicine, and resource-constrained health systems. When sequencing budgets are tight, labs frequently default to targeted gene panels that may miss variants outside a predefined scope, potentially delaying or altogether preventing a molecular diagnosis. A methodology that credibly preserves data integrity while lowering the financial threshold for broader sequencing coverage addresses one of the field's most stubborn practical limitations. Rare disease programs, in particular, stand to benefit from expanded access to a more complete genomic picture without proportionally expanded costs. The approach also aligns with growing interest from payers and health systems in demonstrating value-based returns on genomic investments, making cost-efficiency arguments increasingly central to laboratory procurement and reimbursement conversations.

## What's Next

For genomics laboratories evaluating their sequencing platforms and assay menus, blended genome–exome sequencing warrants close attention in upcoming validation and technology assessment cycles. Vendors and academic sequencing centers will likely move quickly to benchmark the approach against existing workflows, and regulatory bodies may be called upon to clarify whether the methodology requires fresh analytical validation documentation under current frameworks. Integration with AI-driven variant interpretation pipelines is another natural next step, as the richer data output from blended approaches could further improve diagnostic yield when paired with machine learning models trained on large, diverse genomic datasets.

As the genetic testing industry intensifies its focus on expanding population-level genomic screening programs, cost-efficient sequencing methodologies like this one could prove foundational to making comprehensive genomic medicine genuinely scalable across both high-income and emerging markets.
